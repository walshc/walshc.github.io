Instruction Exercise Week 4

Introduction

The file instruction4.csv contains the following variables for a random sample households:

  • foodexp: expenditure on food in thousands of euro.
  • hinc: annual income of the household in thousands of euro.
  • nkids: number of children in the household.
  • hsize: number of people in the household.

Exercises

  1. Estimate a model that explains foodexp with hinc, nkids, and hsize. Interpret the coefficients.
  2. Test whether the model is useful at a significance level of 0.01. Use a critical value approach.
  3. Quantify the degree of usefulness. Interpret the result.
  4. Which variables are individually significant at the 0.01 level?
  5. Does a €1,000 increase in the household income on average lead to an increase in food expenditures of more than €220 at the 0.01 level, holding all else constant? Use a p-value approach.
  6. Obtain an interval that contains with 90% probability the food expenditure of a household with two adults living with two children with annual income of €30,000.
  7. Obtain a 90% confidence interval for the mean food expenditure of households with two adults living with two children with annual income of €30,000.

Question 2

To test the usefulness of the model as a whole we perform an F-test:

  • H_0: \beta_1=\beta_2=\beta_3=0 and H_1: at least one of \beta_j\neq 0 for j=1,2,3.
  • Under H_0, F=\frac{SSR/k}{SSE/(n-k-1)}\sim F_{k,n-k-1}.
  • The value of the F-statistic in our sample is f=1662.866:
summary(m)$fstatistic
   value    numdf    dendf 
1662.866    3.000  296.000
  • The critical value is F_{1-\alpha,k,n-k-1}=3.848431.
qf(0.99, 3, m$df.residual)
[1] 3.848431
  • Because the value of the F-test statistic is greater than the critical value, we reject the null hypothesis. The model is useful at the 1% level.

Question 5

A €1,000 increase in the household income on average leading to an increase in food expenditures of more than €220 implies \beta_1>0.22.

  • H_0: \beta_1\leq 0.22 and H_1: \beta_1>0.22.
  • Under H_0, T=(B_1-0.22)/S_{B_1}\sim t_{n-k-1}.
  • The value of the test statistic in our sample is t=3.9075.
b_1 <- coef(summary(m))["hinc", "Estimate"]
s_b_1 <- coef(summary(m))["hinc", "Std. Error"]
(t <- (b_1 - 0.22) / s_b_1)
[1] 3.90748
  • The p-value is:
1 - pt(t, m$df.residual)
[1] 0.00005781563
  • The p-value is smaller than the significance level of 0.01. Therefore we reject the null hypothesis. There is sufficient evidence for the claim.

Question 6

Note that hinc is in €1,000s, so €30,000 is written as 30. With two adults and two children, the household size is 4. We create a one-observation dataframe df_p with this household:

df_p <- data.frame(hinc = 30, nkids = 2, hsize = 4)

We then use the predict() function with our model and df_p:

predict(m, df_p, interval = "prediction", level = 0.9)
       fit      lwr      upr
1 8.652953 7.281879 10.02403

With 90% probability, a given household with €30,000 income, 2 children and 2 adults, will have food expenditure between €7,281.88 and €10,024.03.

Question 7

We can use the same df_p as before. We only need to change the interval to a confidence interval because now we want an interval for the mean food expenditure of such households, rather than an interval that contains the food expenditure of a single household with 90% probability.

predict(m, df_p, interval = "confidence", level = 0.9)
       fit   lwr      upr
1 8.652953 8.542 8.763907

We are 90% confident that the mean household food expenditure for households with €30,000 income, 2 children and 2 adults is between €8,542.00 and €8.763.91.
